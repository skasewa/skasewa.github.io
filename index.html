Shallow Review of AI Governance
for Catastrophic & Existential Risk

A structured catalog of research agendas, papers, and organizations working on AI governance specifically aimed at preventing catastrophic and existential outcomes from advanced AI systems.

Draft / v0.3: This is an early-stage catalog, modeled after the Shallow Review of Technical AI Safety. It is deliberately shallow — brief descriptions rather than deep analysis. Coverage is incomplete and likely reflects the biases of its sources. Corrections and additions are welcome.
Categories

    Methodology

    What is this?

    This is a shallow review of AI governance research focused on catastrophic and existential risks from AI. It is modeled after Arb Research's Shallow Review of Technical AI Safety, which catalogs 800+ papers and 80+ research agendas in technical AI safety.

    "Shallow" means we provide brief descriptions and categorization rather than deep critical analysis. The goal is legibility — making it easy to see what work exists, who is doing it, and how different efforts relate to each other.

    Scope

    We include work that meets all of the following criteria:

    • Governance-focused: The work concerns policy, institutions, regulation, strategy, or coordination — not purely technical alignment research.
    • X-risk relevant: The work is motivated by or directly relevant to preventing catastrophic or existential outcomes from AI, not only near-term harms like bias or job displacement (though these may overlap).
    • Substantive: The work presents research, analysis, or concrete proposals — not just opinion pieces or news coverage.

    Categorization

    We organize work into 10 categories and multiple research agendas within each. Categories are based on the primary governance lever or domain:

    1. Compute Governance — hardware as a governance lever
    2. International Governance — treaties, institutions, and coordination
    3. Corporate Governance — lab-level policies and structures
    4. Regulatory Frameworks — national and regional legislation
    5. Misuse Prevention — CBRN, autonomous weapons, dual-use
    6. Evaluations & Auditing — third-party assessment and transparency
    7. Strategic Analysis — geopolitics, race dynamics, scenarios
    8. Post-AGI Governance — power concentration, lock-in, civilizational resilience
    9. China & Geopolitics — bilateral dynamics, Global South
    10. Field Building — landscape surveys, talent, research infrastructure

    Known gaps

    The following areas are underrepresented or missing and should be added in future versions:

    • Insurance & liability frameworks — tort liability, mandatory insurance proposals
    • Democratic governance of AI — citizen assemblies, public deliberation, participatory approaches
    • Compute threshold analysis — how the 10^25/10^26 FLOP thresholds were derived and their adequacy

    Limitations

    • Incomplete coverage: AI governance research is published across law reviews, policy briefs, white papers, government reports, think tank publications, and academic venues. No single catalog can capture everything.
    • Selection bias: This review draws heavily from the EA/rationalist community, GovAI, and anglophone policy research. Work in other languages, from government agencies, or from mainstream IR/political science is underrepresented.
    • Categorization is imperfect: Many papers span multiple categories. We assign papers to their primary category but acknowledge overlap.
    • Shallow means shallow: We do not assess quality, correctness, or impact of individual papers.
    • Not peer-reviewed: This catalog itself has not been through formal review.

    How to contribute

    If you know of papers, agendas, or organizations that should be included, please open an issue or pull request on the project repository. We especially welcome additions from underrepresented areas: Global South perspectives, non-English work, government-internal research, and mainstream political science and international relations scholarship.

    About

    Motivation

    The Shallow Review of Technical AI Safety by Arb Research is an invaluable resource for understanding the landscape of technical safety research. But technical safety is only half the picture. Even if we solve alignment perfectly, governance determines whether those solutions are adopted, enforced, and sustained.

    AI governance research aimed at catastrophic and existential risks is fragmented across many organizations, venues, and traditions. There was no single place to see the whole landscape. This project attempts to fill that gap.

    Key organizations

    The following organizations produce substantial AI governance research relevant to catastrophic and existential risk:

    Core x-risk governance research

    • GovAI (Centre for the Governance of AI) — Oxford-based research center, arguably the single largest dedicated AI governance research group focused on existential risk. Led by Ben Garfinkel.
    • MIRI TGT (MIRI Technical Governance Team) — MIRI's governance arm, most explicitly extinction-focused. Advocates for global "Off Switch" infrastructure.
    • CLTR (Centre for Long-Term Resilience) — UK-based think tank. Strong on risk governance at frontier AI labs, the Three Lines Model, and loss-of-control monitoring.
    • IAPS (Institute for AI Policy and Strategy) — DC-based. Strong on compute governance, chip controls, and agent governance. Runs an influential AI policy fellowship.
    • CAIS (Center for AI Safety) — Research and advocacy on catastrophic AI risks. Published the 2023 "Statement on AI Risk" signed by hundreds of researchers.
    • FLI (Future of Life Institute) — Publishes the AI Safety Index, ran the 2023 Pause letter campaign, active in autonomous weapons and EU policy advocacy.
    • Convergence Analysis — Produces foundational comparative reports on AI governance strategies and regulatory landscapes. AI model registries work directly influenced US policy.
    • SI (Simon Institute for Longterm Governance) — Geneva-based, focuses on building multilateral AI governance capacity, particularly at the UN level.

    National security & policy research

    • CSET (Center for Security and Emerging Technology) — Georgetown. Empirical, evidence-driven policy research on AI and national security. The AGORA governance document archive is a key resource.
    • RAND Corporation — Extensive work on AI security, bioweapons misuse risk, historical governance analogues, and frontier AI model security.
    • CNAS (Center for a New American Security) — DC think tank. Work on AI and national security strategy, autonomous weapons, and technology competition.
    • Carnegie Endowment — International affairs think tank with growing AI governance program, particularly on global governance architecture.

    AI lab safety & evaluation

    • METR (Model Evaluation and Threat Research) — Conducts pre-deployment capability evaluations for frontier labs. Published the comprehensive comparison of frontier safety policies.
    • Apollo Research — Research on AI deception, scheming evaluation, and alignment faking in frontier models.
    • UK AISI (UK AI Safety Institute) — Government body conducting pre-deployment evaluations and safety research. Established after Bletchley Park summit.

    Data & trends

    • Epoch AI — Collects and analyzes data on compute trends, algorithmic progress, and AI development trajectories. Essential background for compute governance.
    • MIT AI Risk Initiative — Runs the AI Governance Mapping project analyzing 1,000+ governance documents.

    Multi-stakeholder & standards

    • PAI (Partnership on AI) — Multi-stakeholder initiative. Foundation model deployment guidance and governance priorities.
    • OECD AI Policy Observatory — Tracks 1,000+ AI policy initiatives across 69 countries. The OECD AI Principles are a key soft-law instrument.
    • Stanford HAI (Human-Centered AI) — Publishes the annual AI Index and policy research at the intersection of AI and society.

    Relationship to the original Shallow Review

    This project is inspired by but not affiliated with Arb Research's Shallow Review of Technical AI Safety. We adopt the "shallow review" format — brief, structured, catalog-style — but apply it to a different domain (governance rather than technical safety).
