# Discard Log: Papers and Organizations Excluded

This log documents papers and organizations that were considered but excluded from the Shallow Review, with reasoning.

## Papers Removed

### Removed: "Compute Trends Across Three Eras of Machine Learning" (Sevilla, Heim et al., 2022)
**Reason:** Purely empirical/technical paper measuring compute growth. Provides important background data for compute governance work but is not itself governance research. Doesn't propose policy interventions or analyze governance mechanisms. Epoch AI's data work is referenced in org descriptions instead.

### Removed: "Statement on AI Risk" (CAIS, 2023)
**Reason:** A single sentence ("Mitigating the risk of extinction from AI should be a global priority...") signed by researchers. Important as a political signal but does not meet the "substantive research, analysis, or concrete proposals" inclusion criterion. Referenced in org descriptions instead.

### Removed: "CSET AGORA: AI Governance and Regulatory Archive" (CSET, 2024)
**Reason:** A database/archive of governance documents, not a research paper or analysis. Valuable tool but not substantive research. Referenced in methodology as a data source.

### Removed: "Keeping Top AI Talent in the United States" (Zwetsloot et al., CSET, 2019)
**Reason:** US immigration policy paper focused on talent retention. Tangential to x-risk governance — addresses competitiveness, not catastrophic risk prevention. CSET's more x-risk-relevant work is well-represented elsewhere.

### Removed: Fellowship program listings (GovAI Fellowship, IAPS Fellowship, MIRI TGT Fellowship, ERA Fellowships)
**Reason:** These are program descriptions, not research papers. Important for field-building but don't meet the "substantive research" criterion. All are referenced in the About page's organization descriptions instead.

### Removed: "Algorithmic Progress in Language Models" (Ho, Besiroglu et al., Epoch AI, 2024)
**Reason:** Technical ML paper about training efficiency gains. Not governance research. Removed in v0.2.

## Organizations Not Included (with reasoning)

### AI Now Institute — INCLUDED but with caveats
AI Now does important work on tech industry power concentration, but their framing is primarily about near-term harms (labor, surveillance, bias) rather than catastrophic/existential risk. One paper included; their broader body of work is more adjacent than core.

### Brookings AI Initiative — NOT INCLUDED as core org
Brookings produces useful policy analysis but their AI work is broad (economic impacts, workforce, general governance) rather than focused on catastrophic risk. The "Network Architecture for Global AI Policy" paper is included under China & Geopolitics but Brookings isn't listed as a core org.

### SIPRI — NOT INCLUDED
SIPRI (Stockholm International Peace Research Institute) works on autonomous weapons governance, which is relevant, but their output on AI-specific x-risk governance is thin. Would include if we expand the autonomous weapons section.

### Rethink Priorities — NOT INCLUDED
Rethink Priorities has a small AI governance research team but limited public output specifically on x-risk governance as of early 2026. Worth monitoring.

### CIFAR — NOT INCLUDED
CIFAR's Pan-Canadian AI Strategy is important but focuses on AI innovation ecosystem, not catastrophic risk governance specifically.

### Various corporate AI ethics teams (Google, Microsoft, Meta) — NOT INCLUDED
Large tech company "Responsible AI" teams focus on near-term harms (bias, fairness, transparency) rather than catastrophic/existential risk. The lab safety frameworks (RSP, Preparedness Framework, Frontier Safety Framework) are included under Corporate Governance, but general responsible AI programs are not.

### Partnership on AI (PAI) — DOWNGRADED from core to multi-stakeholder
Industry-dominated organization that avoids taking strong positions on hard governance questions. Their foundation model deployment guidance is useful but they don't push the frontier on x-risk governance. Listed under multi-stakeholder section.

### AI Now Institute — NOT INCLUDED as core org
AI Now does important work on tech industry power concentration, but their framing is explicitly hostile to the x-risk/existential risk framing. Their focus is near-term harms (labor, surveillance, bias). One paper included for power concentration analysis.

### Ada Lovelace Institute — NOT INCLUDED
Focused on current-generation AI harms (bias, fairness, procurement). Their UK regulation analysis was useful but the organization's mission doesn't center catastrophic risk.

### CIFAR — NOT INCLUDED
Research funder, not a governance research org. The Pan-Canadian AI Strategy is innovation-focused.

### Encode Justice — NOT INCLUDED
Youth climate-activism-style mobilization around AI ethics. Not producing substantive x-risk governance research.

### Center for AI and Digital Policy (CAIDP) — NOT INCLUDED
Democratic values and human rights framing. Useful for general AI governance but not focused on catastrophic/existential risk.

### ITU — NOT INCLUDED
Telecom standards body. Their AI governance work is procedural/standards-focused, not x-risk-relevant.

### Center for AI Policy (CAIP) — NOTED AS DEFUNCT
Was the only dedicated US congressional x-risk advocacy org. Closed in 2025. A significant gap in the ecosystem.
